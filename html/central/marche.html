Geography

Marche is a region in central Italy located between the Apennine Mountains and the Adriatic Sea. Known for its beautiful combination of mountains, rolling hills, and coastline, Marche offers a diverse landscape that includes natural parks, historic towns, and sandy beaches. Compared to some of Italy's more famous regions, it is often considered a hidden gem because of its rich culture, scenic beauty, and relatively lower number of tourists. The Marche region is bordered by Emilia-Romagna and the republic of San Marino to the north, Tuscany and Umbria to the west, Lazio to the southwest, Abruzzo to the south, and the Adriatic Sea to the east.

Marche is also known for its medieval hill towns, Renaissance heritage, and strong traditions in agriculture, craftsmanship, and cuisine. Its unique blend of coastal and mountain environments makes it one of the most varied and picturesque regions in central Italy.

The region is divided into 5 provinces: Ancona, Ascoli Piceno, Fermo, Macerata, and Pesaro and Urbino.

Capital (and largest city): Ancona

Climate

The region of central Italy features a highly diverse climate defined by its distinct geography: a mild, coastal Mediterranean climate along the Adriatic Sea, and a colder, continental climate in the rugged inland mountainous areas.

The Coast (Adriatic Side)

Summers: Warm and sunny, with average highs in the upper 20s°C (82°F-85°F) in July and August. Sea breezes help moderate the humidity.

Winters: Mild and generally short, with daytime temperatures hovering around 10°C-13°C (50°F–55°F), though occasional cold winds from the north can sweep through.

Rainfall: Driest during the summer months (about 35-40 mm of rain in July).

The Inland (Apennines & Hills)

Summers: Warm but dry, offering cooler evenings that provide natural relief from the daytime heat.

Winters: Long, cold, and often snowy in the higher, mountainous altitudes (like Monte Vettore).

Rainfall: More frequent than the coast, with total precipitation ranging from 1,000 to 1,500 mm annually.

Spring and Autumn

Spring (April and May): A beautiful transition period with daytime temperatures rising into the comfortable 15°C-20°C (low-to-mid 60s°F) range, making it ideal for hiking and exploring local towns.

Autumn (October and November): The wettest part of the year, especially November. It brings mild, comfortable temperatures early on that steadily drop into the rainy and cool late fall.

Topography

The Marche are rugged lands; hills and mountains dominate the landscape. The highest points are in the central Apennines in the region's western districts. The tallest mountain, at 2,476 m (8,123 feet), is Monte Vettore in the Sibillini range along the border with Umbria. Once the land reaches the sea, the Marche landscape is rolling hills or dramatic cliffs cascading into the Adriatic, such as at the Monte Conero, just south of Ancona and the coastline northwest of Pesaro.

It is a very fertile region, especially in fruit-trees, olives and vines; and it has been, both in ancient and modern times, a populous district, containing many small towns though no great cities. The coast is almost an almost unbroken straight line with few ports, with the main exception of Ancona.

Origins

Did you know?

The name Marche comes from the medieval term for “border territories” or “marches,” as the region once marked a frontier area between different political powers in central Italy. This historical role is still reflected in its name today, which is always used in the plural form.

Marche was originally inhabited by the Picentes, an ancient Italic people whose culture flourished during the Iron Age. In the 4th century BC, parts of the region were settled by the Senones, a Gallic tribe. After the Battle of Sentinum, the Romans gained control of the region and established important colonies connected by major roads such as the Via Flaminia and Via Salaria.

Following the fall of the Western Roman Empire, Marche was ruled by several powers, including the Goths, the Byzantines, and the Lombards. In the late 8th century, the region was conquered by Charlemagne and became part of the Frankish Empire. During the Middle Ages, many cities developed as independent communes, while Ancona became a prosperous maritime republic and trading center on the Adriatic Sea.

During the Renaissance, powerful families such as the Montefeltro ruled parts of the region, particularly the city of Urbino, which became an important center of art and culture. After the decline of these local states, Marche became firmly integrated into the Papal States.

In the 19th century, the region briefly came under Napoleonic rule before returning to the Papal States. In 1860, Marche joined the newly unified Italy following a plebiscite. More recently, the region has experienced several major earthquakes, including those of 2016, but continues to preserve its rich historical and cultural heritage.

Population and Major Cities

Population

As of 2026, the total population is 1.4 million people.

The region covers an area of 9,346 km² and a population density of 158.3/km².

Major Cities (as of 2026)

1. Ancona

Ancona is a historic port city on Italy's Adriatic coast, renowned for its ancient maritime heritage, Roman and Greek architecture, and stunning coastal landscapes. Known as "the Doric City" due to its Greek founding, it serves as a vital gateway for travelers and a beautiful base for the Marche region.

Population: 100,151 people

2. Pesaro

Pesaro is a charming Italian coastal city on the Adriatic, internationally renowned as a UNESCO Creative City of Music and the birthplace of the legendary opera composer Gioachino Rossini. It is celebrated for its vast sandy beaches, rich Renaissance history, and premier cycling culture.

Population: 95,270 people

3. Fano

Fano is a charming coastal city in Italy's Marche region, best known for its ancient Roman history, its popular Adriatic seaside resorts, and for hosting one of the oldest and most vibrant Carnivals in Italy.

Population: 59,871 people

4. San Benedetto del Tronto

San Benedetto del Tronto is a vibrant coastal resort town in Italy’s Marche region, famed for its palm-fringed "Riviera delle Palme," award-winning beaches, and bustling fishing port. It offers a mix of seaside relaxation and rich maritime culture.

Population: 46,976 people

Landmarks

Basilica della Santa Casa (Sanctuary of the Holy House of Loreto)

The Basilica della Santa Casa is one of the most important pilgrimage sites in Italy. It is believed to house the Holy House of the Virgin Mary, making it a major religious destination for centuries. The basilica features impressive Renaissance and Gothic architecture, and it attracts visitors from around the world for both its spiritual and artistic significance.

Urbino

Urbino is one of the most important Renaissance cities in Italy and is recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The entire historic centre is considered a landmark because of its well-preserved architecture, including palaces, churches, and fortified walls that reflect its role as a cultural capital during the Renaissance under the Montefeltro family. The city is also closely linked to the artist Raphael, who was born there, making Urbino a major centre of Italian art history.

Flag

The official flag of the Marche region in Italy consists of a pristine white field featuring the regional coat of arms in the center, frequently with the words "Regione Marche" in green positioned above it.

Adoption

Like many of the other regions' flags, it was adopted on 4 November 1995 for Armed Forces Day of Italy.

Symbolism

The flag of Marche features a stylized woodpecker forming the letter “M” on a shield. This design was chosen to represent the region in a modern and symbolic way, combining identity and geography into one simple emblem.

The woodpecker comes from the ancient Picentes, the early people who lived in the region. In their tradition, the woodpecker was a sacred animal and a guiding symbol, which is why it later became linked to the name and identity of the area. Today, it represents Marche's historical roots and cultural heritage.

The modern flag of Marche is inspired by the region's ancient history, especially the symbol of the woodpecker linked to the Picentes, the earliest known inhabitants of the area. Over time, different parts of Marche also developed their own historical flags, including the Duchy of Urbino and the Republic of Ancona, which both used distinct designs reflecting their political independence and strong cultural identities. Today's regional flag combines these deep historical roots into a single modern symbol representing unity and heritage.

Languages

The main language spoken in Marche is Italian, which is used in schools, government, and everyday communication. However, the region also has several local dialects that reflect its historical and cultural diversity.

One of the most important is the Central Marchigiano dialect, which is part of the broader group of central Italian dialects. It is mainly spoken in everyday informal situations and varies slightly from town to town. While Italian remains dominant, these dialects are an important part of local identity and cultural heritage in the region.

Food

The cuisine of Marche is known for being simple, rustic, and based on local ingredients from both the mountains and the Adriatic coast. It combines inland traditions like truffles, legumes, and meats with coastal seafood dishes, creating a balanced mix of flavours. Many recipes are traditional and homemade, reflecting the region's strong rural heritage and focus on seasonal cooking.

Primo (First Course)

Vincisgrassi

Vincisgrassi is a rich baked pasta dish similar to lasagna, layered with pasta sheets, meat sauce, and sometimes béchamel. It is one of the most famous traditional dishes in Marche and is often prepared for special occasions. The recipe dates back centuries and reflects the region's hearty inland cuisine.

Secondo (Second Course)

Porchetta

Porchetta is a savoury roasted pork dish seasoned with herbs such as rosemary, garlic, and fennel. It is slow-cooked until the meat is tender and the skin is crispy. In Marche, it is especially popular as street food and at festivals.

Dolce (Dessert)

Cicerchiata

Cicerchiata is a festive dessert made of small fried dough balls mixed with honey and sometimes decorated with almonds or sprinkles. It is similar to other Italian carnival sweets and is especially popular during holidays like Carnival and Easter. The dessert is sweet, crunchy, and often shared in large groups, reflecting the region's tradition of communal celebrations.

Regional Spotlight

Truffles

Truffles are one of the most prized food products from Marche, especially in its inland forested areas. They are a type of rare fungus that grows underground and are collected with the help of trained dogs. Marche produces both black and white truffles, which are highly valued in Italian cuisine for their strong, earthy aroma and flavour. They are often used in simple dishes like pasta, eggs, and meat to enhance taste without overpowering the ingredients.

Art and Music

The City of Urbino

The region of Marche is known for its strong Renaissance heritage and historic artistic centres, especially the city of Urbino. During the Renaissance, Urbino became a major cultural hub under the Montefeltro family, attracting artists, architects, and scholars who helped shape Italian art. The region is also known for its religious art, frescoes, and well-preserved medieval towns that reflect centuries of artistic development.

Gioachino Rossini

One of the most famous musical figures linked to Marche is Gioachino Rossini, born in Pesaro. He is one of the greatest opera composers in history, known for works such as The Barber of Seville. Rossini’s music is famous for its lively energy, elegant melodies, and strong influence on Italian opera, making him a key cultural figure from the region.
