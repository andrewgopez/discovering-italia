Geography

Umbria is a landlocked region located in central Italy and is often called the “green heart of Italy” because of its rolling hills, forests, and countryside. The region is characterized by a landscape of valleys, mountains, and fertile plains, with the Tiber River flowing through much of its territory. Umbria is also home to Lake Trasimeno, one of the largest lakes in Italy, which is surrounded by scenic hills and historic towns. Its natural beauty, mild climate, and abundance of rural landscapes make it one of Italy’s most picturesque regions.

Did you know?

Umbria is the only region in peninsular Italy that has neither a coastline nor an international border. Because of this, it is often called “the green heart of Italy.” It is located entirely within central Italy and is completely surrounded by other Italian regions, bordering Tuscany to the north, Lazio to the west and south, and Marche to the east.

It is made up of 2 provinces: Perugia and Terni

Capital (and largest city): Perugia

Climate

The climate varies mainly according to the altitude, and is practically Mediterranean in the lowlands, while it gradually becomes more continental and colder as the altitude increases. In winter, fog can form in periods of stable weather, or the tramontana, the cold north wind, can blow on other occasions. In summer it is very hot during the day, with peaks as high as 40 °C at low altitudes, but at night the temperature drops a lot, and it can get cool at hill altitudes. Precipitation is moderate, as it is generally between 27 and 43 inches per year.

The rainiest season is autumn, when sometimes, usually in November but sometimes also in December, the Tiber river or one of its tributaries can overflow. The driest season is summer, when the weather is often good, though thunderstorms can break out in the afternoon. Snowfalls are rare at low altitudes, while they become more frequent and abundant as the altitude increases.

Topography

Landlocked, the region encompasses Lake Trasimeno, the fourth largest natural lake in Italy, and Lake Piediluco. The river Tiber crosses the region from north to south, and into it flow a number of Umbrian rivers. One of these, the Velino, forms the 165 m-high Marmore waterfall. Uplands and mountains cover almost the entire region's territory; Umbria is crossed by the Umbria-Marche Apennines in the eastern part of its region. The main lowlands are in the valley of the Tiber, in the triangle between Perugia, Foligno and Spoleto, and are largely planted with tobacco. Umbria has a large number of caves rich in cave deposits, stalactites and stalagmites.

Origins

Umbria gained its name from the Umbri, an ancient tribe of people who settled in the area around the 6th century BC. However, this is not the earliest civilization to settle here, with archaeological proof dating back to the Bronze and Iron Ages. The Umbri tribe flourished early in Italian history, building civilized towns to the east of the region, including Spoleto, Gubbio, Città di Castello and the world-renowned Assisi.

Some say the Umbri were originally of Celtic origin, and had a distinct language that was both written and spoken. In any case, whilst the Umbri resided to the east of the river Tevere building up their empire, their rivals the Etruscans to the west established towns such as Perugia and Orvieto and created powerful city-states. The two conflicting tribes came to war when the Umbri land was invaded by the Etruscan people, forcing the Umbrians from their homes. However, the two opposing tribes joined forces in 295BC in an unsuccessful bid to drive away the powerful Roman Empire who sought to take their lands.

Etruria controlled Rome until about 500 B.C., when the Romans rebelled and began their own now world-famous conquest of progressively larger reaches of tribes and lands. Rome started making gains on both sides of the Tiber river, in both Umbri and Etruscan lands. Both the Etruscans and the Umbri finally capitulated to the Romans at almost exactly the same time, around 264-260 B.C. There had been 12 principal cities in Etruria, with the Etruscan capital being Velzna.

The two principal suspects have always been Orvieto (a city in modern-day Umbria) and Bolsena (a city just across the border in Lazio). The citizens of Umbria becoming citizens of Rome in 90BC and although it began a long period of peace in the land that was only disturbed when thriving Roman civilization was invaded by the Barbarians.

The Barbarians brought with them suffering, famine and disease, as well as the economic and cultural decline that spread across the region. By about 600-700 AD, control of the Italian peninsula had been consolidated in large territories. The Lombards to the north and the Byzantines to the east fought heavily for central Italy.

The towns of Perugia and Orvieto passed back and forth from the control of one to the other even from year to year. The political power-gap of the Middle Ages was finally satisfied by the Lombards who created the Duchy of Spoleto from the 6th century up until the 13th century when Umbria became part of the Papal State.

The region was later divided into independent city-states, each thriving and prospering through their business and art, and supportive of their separate rules by either the Pope or the Holy Roman Empire. By mid-14th Century, however, the supremacy of the Pope ruled the area, and the papacy ruled the region uncontested until the end of the 18th century.

Indeed, Umbria is a very Medieval region – boasting the greatest number of medieval villages in Italy, with centuries-old fortresses, including the imposing Rocca Paolina, commissioned by Pope Paul III as a symbol of papal power over the ancient city of Perugia.  Saint Francis of Assisi (who lived during the Medieval period) was born here, giving rise to the Italian literary tradition and a new type of spirituality linked to fraternity, humility and poverty.

During the Renaissance, Umbria once more occupied a place of honour in the creative arts as the home of the Umbrian school of painting, with such masters as Perugino and Pinturicchio.

After the French Revolution and the French conquest of Italy, Umbria became part of the ephemeral Roman Republic (1798–1799) and later, part of the Napoleonic Empire (1809–1814) under the name of department of Trasimène. After Napoleon's defeat, the Pope regained Umbria and ruled it until 1860. In that year, during Italian Risorgimento, Umbria with Marche and part of Emilia Romagna were annexed by Piedmontese King Victor Emmanuel II, and the people of Perugia destroyed in the same year the Rocca Paolina, symbol of the papal oppression. The region of Umbria, with capital Perugia, became part of the Kingdom of Italy in the following year.

TL;DR: Umbria as an administrative region is a fairly modern concept. It was never a well defined region in and of itself, unlike some other areas of Italy. For example, Tuscany was an independent state for many centuries, the Duchy of Tuscany. Piemonte and Sardinia corresponded to the Kingdom of the Savoy. Instead, Umbria was simply one part of a huge swath of central Italy that stretched from Rome to Bologna, and was under the command of the Pope from the middle ages through the unification of Italy in 1870. 

Population and Major Cities

Population

As of 2026, Umbria's population is 850,627.

In addition, the region covers an area of 8,464 km² and has a population density of 100.5/km².

Major Cities (as of 2026)

1. Perugia

Perugia is the capital of the Umbria region and is known for its rich medieval history, hilltop location, and strong cultural and artistic heritage. The city is famous for its well-preserved historic center, featuring ancient walls, narrow streets, and landmarks such as the Palazzo dei Priori and the Fontana Maggiore. Perugia is also internationally recognized for its chocolate industry, especially the famous Baci Perugina chocolates, and for hosting major cultural events like the Umbria Jazz Festival and the Eurochocolate festival. Its combination of history, food culture, and vibrant events makes it one of Umbria’s most important and visited cities.

Population: 162,384 people

2. Terni

Terni is a city in the Umbria region known for its industrial history and its important role in Italy’s steel production. Often called the “Steel City,” it developed as a major industrial center in the 19th and 20th centuries, especially around the steelworks that remain central to its economy. Terni is also famous for the nearby Cascata delle Marmore, one of the tallest man-made waterfalls in the world, created by the ancient Romans. In addition to its industrial identity, the city has cultural traditions and hosts events such as the celebration of Saint Valentine, who is believed to have been born there.

Population: 106,272 people

3. Foligno

Foligno is a city in the Umbria region known for its historic center, cultural traditions, and strategic location in the Tiber Valley. It has a long history dating back to Roman times and features landmarks such as the Palazzo Trinci, which showcases medieval and Renaissance art. Foligno is also famous for the Quintana, a traditional jousting tournament held twice a year that recreates the atmosphere of the city’s historical past. Today, it serves as an important local hub for commerce and transport while preserving its rich cultural heritage.

Population: 55,262 people

4. Città di Castello

Città di Castello is a city in the northern part of Umbria known for its Renaissance art, historic architecture, and cultural heritage. It developed along the upper Tiber Valley and features well-preserved medieval walls, churches, and palaces that reflect its long history. The city is especially recognized for its connection to the Renaissance artist Raphael, who created some of his early works in the area. Today, Città di Castello is also known for its local craftsmanship, museums, and quiet historic atmosphere, making it an important cultural center in Umbria.

Population: 37,847 people

Landmarks

Lake Trasimeno

Lake Trasimeno is the largest lake in central Italy and one of Umbria’s most important natural landmarks. Surrounded by rolling hills, olive groves, and small historic towns, it offers scenic views and a peaceful atmosphere. The lake is also known for its islands, such as Isola Maggiore, and has played an important role in history, including the famous Battle of Lake Trasimeno during the Roman era.

Basilica of Saint Francis of Assisi

Basilica of Saint Francis of Assisi is one of the most important religious and artistic sites in Italy. Located in the hill town of Assisi, it is dedicated to Saint Francis, the patron saint of Italy. The basilica is famous for its stunning frescoes by Giotto and other medieval artists, and it attracts pilgrims and visitors from around the world as a major spiritual and cultural landmark.

Flag

The emblem is a stylized and graphical representation of the three candles of the city of Gubbio, in red colour fimbriated white on a white rectangular field. The gonfalon is green with the emblem in the middle.

Adoption

The current flag was officially adopted on 18 March 2004, although the emblem and gonfalon had been in use since the 1970s.

Symbolism

The flag of Umbria is inspired by a banner designed by architects Gino and Alberto Anselmi, winners of a 1971 competition, who described the symbolism: We proposed [...] that the emblem and gonfalon would become signs of that territory [Umbria, ed], and thus would reflect the spirit, the tensions, the atmosphere, the colors of those places that still live on in deep medieval values.

Did you know?

The symbol in the center of the flag represents the three candles of the Festa dei Ceri, a traditional festival held every year on May 15 in Gubbio in honour of Saint Ubaldo Baldassini. The “ceri” are large wooden structures carried during a famous race through the town, representing Saint Ubaldo, Saint George, and Saint Anthony, who are the patrons of different local groups. Each team wears specific colours—yellow for Saint Ubaldo, blue for Saint George, and black for Saint Anthony. The celebration is an important cultural event in Umbria, combining religious tradition, history, and strong local identity.

Languages

Umbria is part of a larger band of dialects called Central Italian. It can then be further subdivided into Umbrian: Northern, Northwestern and Viterbese, and Southeastern

Like all the other regions, Umbria does not have a single dialect, but in this region in particular, no common language has developed. As just mentioned, we can divide the dialects in three main areas of influence, but today we are going to look at the center/north-west area and the perugino. The perugino is peculiar because it is much more influence by languages from both Tuscany and Marche and not Romagna as the other areas of influence, and even more ancient Italian words; however, there are words that seem to come directly from German. Perugia’s dialect is very unique, and although not many people speak perugino in their daily lives, a few words of it have made their way into the local vernacular.

Examples:

Gimo! (Italian: Andiamo!) → Let’s go!

Bulo! (Italian: Figo!) → Cool!

Freghi (Italian: Ragazzi) → Guys (in reference to a group of people, male and female)


The Orvietan dialect is different from many others in Italy. It has a very specific connotation that partly includes dialects from Umbria, Tuscany and northern Lazio, but at the same time it is very different from the dialects of close cities like Terni and Perugia. The dialect spoken by Orvietans draws from a more informal Roman dialect that is enriched with many other meanings of the terms and is combined with the Tuscan dialects. It is a true blend of multiple dialects originating from the southern part of Tuscany, all of Umbria, and northern Lazio. The Orvietano dialect is quite complex and intriguing but many native Italians from other regions sometimes find it to be difficult to understand.

Food

Umbrian cuisine is known for its simple, rustic style that focuses on high-quality local ingredients. The region is especially famous for truffles, olive oil, legumes, and cured meats, which are often used in traditional dishes. Pasta such as strangozzi is commonly served with rich sauces like black truffle or tomato-based recipes. Umbria is also known for its pork products, including sausages and porchetta, as well as hearty soups made from beans and lentils. Overall, the food in Umbria reflects its rural landscape and tradition of traditional, home-style cooking.

Primo (First Course)

Strangozzi al Tartufo

Strangozzi al tartufo is a traditional Umbrian pasta dish made with thick, hand-rolled pasta and black truffle sauce. It is simple but rich in flavour, highlighting one of Umbria’s most famous ingredients, the truffle. The dish reflects the region’s rustic cooking style and focus on local products.

Secondo (Second Course)

Cinghiale in Umido

Native to both Tuscany and Umbria, Cinghiale in umido is a traditional wild boar stew cooked slowly with tomatoes, red wine, herbs, and vegetables. It is known for its rich, deep flavour and is often served with pasta or polenta. Wild boar hunting has long been part of Umbria’s rural tradition, making this dish a strong reflection of the region’s countryside cuisine.

Dolce (Dessert)

Rocciata

Rocciata is a traditional Umbrian dessert pastry made with thin dough filled with apples, nuts, cocoa, and spices, then rolled into a spiral and baked. It is especially associated with festive occasions and has a warm, rustic flavour that reflects the region’s home-style baking traditions.

Regional Spotlight

Baci Perugina

Umbria, particularly the city of Perugia, is famous for its long-standing chocolate production. The most well-known product is Baci Perugina, a chocolate made of hazelnut and chocolate filled with a whole hazelnut and a love note inside each wrapper. Created in the early 20th century, it has become one of Italy’s most iconic chocolates and is exported worldwide. Perugia is also known for hosting the Eurochocolate Festival, a major annual event celebrating chocolate culture, which attracts visitors from across the globe.

Art and Music

Art

Umbria is best known for its medieval and early Renaissance religious art, especially frescoes found in churches and historic towns. One of the most important examples is inside the Basilica of Saint Francis of Assisi, where the interior walls are covered with famous fresco cycles, including works attributed to Giotto. These paintings depict scenes from the life of Saint Francis and are considered masterpieces of early Renaissance art. The basilica is one of the most significant artistic and religious sites in Italy, attracting visitors from around the world.

Michele Bravi

Michele Bravi is a singer-songwriter from Città di Castello in Umbria. He rose to national fame after winning X Factor Italia in 2013 and has since become one of Italy’s well-known contemporary pop artists. He is especially famous for songs such as Il Diario degli Errori (Diary of Errors), which became one of his biggest hits after being performed at the Sanremo Music Festival. Known for his emotional lyrics and melodic style, Bravi has helped bring modern Italian pop music to a wide audience while maintaining a connection to his Umbrian roots.

Umbria Jazz Festival

Umbria Jazz Festival is one of the most important jazz festivals in Europe and is held every year in the city of Perugia. Founded in 1973, the festival attracts internationally renowned musicians and thousands of visitors from around the world. While jazz remains its main focus, the event also features performances in genres such as blues, soul, pop, and world music. Concerts take place in historic squares, theaters, and outdoor venues throughout Perugia, creating a vibrant atmosphere that combines music, culture, and the region’s rich heritage.
