Benvenuti in Emilia-Romagna

Geography

Emilia-Romagna is situated in northern Italy between the Apennine Mountains and the Adriatic Sea. It is bordered by is Lombardy and Veneto to the north, Piedmont and Liguria to the west, and Tuscany, Le Marche and the Republic of San Marino to the south.

Fun fact: The region itself evolved from the joining of two historic regions: Emilia and Romagna.

The region is divided into 9 provinces: Bologna, Ferrara, Forlì-Cesena, Modena, Parma, Piacenza, Ravenna, Reggio Emilia, and Rimini

Capital (and largest city): Bologna

Climate

The location of the region marks the division between the colder lands of Northern Italy, in the Appenines (where winter temperatures can reach -15°C (5°F)) and the sunny lands of the South (where temperatures can reach 45°C (113°F)).

Emilia Romagna itself is a region with a mild continental climate. Temperatures during the day are in the range from 6°C (43°F) in January to 30°C (86°F) in July. The least rainy month in Emilia Romagna is July with its 6 days of rain, the rainiest month is April, when it rains 10 days.

Topography

Nearly half of the region (48%) consists of plains while 27% is hilly and 25% mountainous. The region lies below the Appenines and extensive Po river, where most of the land is flat. With the exception of the Po, the region’s main rivers descend from these mountains. Lots of empty expanse and wetlands mark the natural biodiversity of the Po Delta.

Pastoral hills cover the bottom of the region as the land reaches the Apennines, where the sea level begins to rise and eventually breaks 2,000 metres. The central swathe has a rich harvest and is where most regional food staples come from. East of the region are largely treeless and cultivated with wheat.

Industry

With its broad lowland and adequate water supply (from both rainfall and irrigation), Emilia-Romagna is one of the leading agricultural regions of Italy. Wheat, corn (maize), fodder, and sugar beets are the principal crops; vegetables and fruits are also grown in the lowlands and grapes on the Apennine slopes. Livestock raising and dairy farming are extensive, and the region has a large food-processing and food-packing industry.

The manufacture of cars and trucks, farm machinery, chemicals and pharmaceuticals, ceramics, and clothing is important. Small hydroelectric stations on the rivers provide power, and these are connected with the Alpine plants so that interchange at different seasons is possible. The discovery of large deposits of natural gas and of oil gives the region a vital role in the energy economy of Italy.

Finally, Bologna is a communications hub for commerce between northern and southern Italy, and the region is well served by secondary railway lines and highways.

Origins

Pre-Romans: Emilia-Romagna was part of the Etruscan world and subsequently the Celtic Gauls. For three hundred years from the 9th century BC, the land was colonized by the Etruscans. During this period, numerous centers including Cesena, Parma, Piacenza, and Modena arose.

Roman Era: For the first thousand years AD, during the Roman Empire and beyond, it was an important region for trade and religion, playing host to many monasteries.

After the Fall of Roman Empire: In the 6th century, the region of Emilia became part of the kingdom of Lombardy, founded by the Germanic Lombards. When Charlemagne overthrew the dynasty, Romagna came under Byzantine rule, with Ravenna the capital of the Byzantine Empire. In the 8th century, Charlemagne's son gifted the region to the Pope. It be would under Papal rule for the next few centuries. Romagna was contested between various powers, including the Papal States and the Holy Roman Empire because of its fertile lands and key cities like Ravenna.

Renaissance Era: Many cities in the current region actually belonged to either: Its own Duchy (a kingdom ruled by a duke or duchess) Its own City-State (a region independently ruled by a major city, governed by elected leaders or ruling families) Under Papal rule (lands controlled and ruled by the Pope)

The House of Este dynasty then rose to prominence. Their support of the arts left behind a creative legacy. Culture then thrived in the 15th and 16th centuries. Tourists flock to the Renaissance cities of Modena, Parma and Ferrara.

Napoleon rule: In the 1800s, Emilia then came under Napoleon rule and ultimately, French control. The urge for Italian independence had already started to swell at this point. The region then officially became part of the Kingdom of Italy in 1861. Since then, it saw a complete restructuring of the agriculture. This ended in the rise of an agricultural proletariat for the region and mainstream prominence of the class conflicts. The workforce chambers and institutions then formed the backbone of the modern Emilia-Romagna.

Post-WWI and Post-WWII: After WWI, the region was characterized by social and political tension, resulting in unfortunate levels of violence. The Second World War saw devastation. Bologna in particular was severely damaged. But from the ashes grew hope. The region then became the main character of the Italian economic miracle. It witnessed the hegemony of the politically left parties, which gave rise to what's known as 'The Emilian Model'. In 1948, the region of Emilia merged with the newly autonomous Romagna. Modern day Emilia-Romagna is still left wing, having been since the Second World War. Plans to split Emilia and Romagna, as well as reduce the nine regions to four, have been raised but ultimately dropped.

Population and Major Cities

Population

As of 2024, the population is 4.4 million people.

The region covers an area of 22,501 km². In addition, it has a a population density of about 198 people per km².

Major Cities (as of 2021)

1. Bologna

The capital of Emilia-Romagna, Bologna is globally celebrated as the culinary capital of Italy (La Grassa), home to the world’s oldest continuously operating university (La Dotta), and famous for its sprawling, UNESCO-listed medieval porticoes and terracotta-red architecture (La Rossa).

Population: 394,463 people

2. Parma

Known globally as the capital of the Italian "Food Valley", Parma is famous for its world-class gastronomy, rich musical heritage, and beautifully preserved Romanesque architecture

Population: 200,218 people

3. Modena

Modena is known globally as the birthplace of traditional balsamic vinegar, iconic luxury sports cars like Ferrari and Maserati, and culinary masterpieces like Parmigiano Reggiano and tortellini. It is also famous for its UNESCO-listed architecture and rich operatic heritage.

Population: 187,977 people

4. Reggio Emilia

This city is primarily known as the birthplace of the Italian flag, a pioneer in world-renowned early childhood education, and a powerhouse for authentic Italian gastronomy.

Population: 169,803 people

Landmarks

Museo Ferrari (Ferrari Museum)

Emilia-Romagna is well known for its strong automotive heritage, especially through the Ferrari Museum, which showcases the history, design, and innovation of one of the world’s most famous luxury sports car brands. Located in Maranello, it is the most iconic of the region’s motor museums and attracts visitors interested in Ferrari’s Formula 1 legacy and legendary road cars. The region is also home to the Lamborghini Museum, which highlights Lamborghini’s high-performance supercars, and the Ducati Museum, which celebrates Ducati’s history in motorcycles and racing.

Parmesan Museum

The Parmesan Museum is dedicated to the history and production of Parmigiano Reggiano, one of Italy’s most famous cheeses. Located in the Parma region, it showcases traditional cheesemaking methods, aging techniques, and the strict production standards that give the cheese its unique flavor and quality. Visitors can learn how it is made from local milk and aged for long periods to develop its rich taste and texture. The museum highlights how Parmesan cheese became a symbol of Italian culinary heritage, craftsmanship, and regional pride.
