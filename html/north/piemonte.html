Benvenuti in Piemonte (Piedmont)

Geography

Piemonte is located in Northern Italy (Italia Settentrionale) or more specifically, the northwestern part of Italy. It is bordered by France (west), Switzerland and Valle D'Aosta (north), Lombardia (east), and Liguria (south).

Significance of the name: The term "piedmont' means "at the foot of the mountain" ("piede" meaning foot; "monte" meaning mountain).

The region is divided into 8 provinces: Alessandria, Asti, Biella, Cuneo, Novara, Torino (Turin), Verbano-Cusio-Ossola and Vercelli

Capital (and largest city): Torino

Climate

During the winter, temperatures are around 2°C (35°F) and it is one of the snowiest regions of Italy.

During the spring, the weather is pleasant but rainy, with May being the wettest month.

During the summer, on average, temperatures go to 22°C (72°F).

During the autumn months, there is a gradual drop from 16°C (61°F) in September to 6°C (43°F) in November.

Topography

About 40% of the region is mountainous. The rest is made up of hills and plains, with rivers flowing into the Po Valley. The Apennine Mountains begin in the southeast.

Origins

Early Inhabitants (c. 3000 BCE)

The region was first inhabited by the Celts and the Ticino rivers. They were succeeded by the Ligurians and the Celtic descendants, including the Taurins.

Roman Era

By the 1st century BCE, the region caught the attention of the Romans. They used it to control the Alpine passes of Valle D’Aosta, which was a key access point to Gaul (present-day France, Belgium, Luxembourg and parts of Switzerland). In 28 BC, Julius Caesar ordered the city of Julia Augusta Taurinorum, which is now present-day Torino to be built.

Fall of the Roman Empire

After the fall of the Roman Empire, in the 5th century, Piemonte was controlled by the Germanic people who settled in the area, before joining the Carolingian Empire in the 8th century. In the 11th century, the region was subdivided into the Marquisate of Monferrato, which ended when the House of Savoy took over in the 14th century, gaining influence via the Alpine passes.

Post-Napoleon

In 1706, following the Siege of Turin against the French, Victor Amadeus II was crowned King of the Kingdom of Sardegna, which at the time, Piemonte was included. This was a prosperous period for the Turin court. After a brief period of Napoleonic rule, in 1814, Victor Emmanuel I regained control of the kingdom and began unifying the region with the rest of Italy, which ended on March 17th, 1861.

Population and Major Cities

Population

As of 2024, the total population is 4.3 million people.

The region covers an area of 25,400 km², making it the second largest area after Siciliy (Sicilia). In addition, it has a a population density of about 167 people per km².

Most Populated Cities (as of 2026)

1. Torino (Turin)

The capital city of the region, it is known for its rich royal history, grand Baroque architecture, and car manufacturing.

Population: 855,654 people

2. Novara

Known for its towering Basilica of San Gaudenzio, status as a major agricultural hub for rice production, and rich gastronomic traditions including its namesake biscuits and Gorgonzola cheese

Population: 103,238 people

3. Alessandria

Known for its luxury hat-making heritage (the iconic Borsalino brand), its massive 18th-century military fortress, and its proximity to the historic Napoleon-era Battle of Marengo site

Population: 93,409 people

4. Asti

Known internationally for its wines, particularly Asti Spumante (a sweet sparkling wine) and Barbera d'Asti (a rich red wine). Besides its winemaking heritage, Asti is famous for the Palio di Asti, one of Italy's oldest horse races, dating back to the 13th century.

Population: 73,604 people

Major Landmarks

Piemonte is internationally famous for landmarks spanning royal palaces, sacred mountaintops, and historic wine cellars. The region’s standout architectural and historical treasures are shaped by its legacy as the cradle of the House of Savoy and the birthplace of unified Italy.

La Reggia di Venaria Reale (Palace of Venaria)

The Reggia di Venaria Reale (Palace of Venaria) is a grand 17th-century former royal residence and gardens located in Venaria Reale, about 14 kilometers northwest of Torino, Italy. Often dubbed the "Versailles of Italy," it is one of the largest and most spectacular Baroque architectural complexes in the world.

Mole Antonelliana

The Mole Antonelliana is the iconic architectural landmark of Torino, Italy. Designed by Alessandro Antonelli in 1863, it is the world's tallest unreinforced brick building (167.5 meters / 550 feet) and currently houses the spectacular National Museum of Cinema.

Sacra di San Michele (Saint Michael's Abbey)

The Sacra di San Michele is an ancient, imposing religious complex perched atop Mount Pirchiriano in the Susa Valley of Piemonte, Italy. Constructed between 983 and 987 AD, it is the symbolic monument of the region.

Museo Nazionale dell'Automobile (National Automobile Museum)

The Museo Nazionale dell'Automobile (often called MAUTO) is one of the world's most prestigious and historic car museums. Located in Torino, Italy—the heart of the Italian automotive industry—it showcases the evolution of the automobile from 19th-century steam carriages to modern Formula 1 racers.
