Geography

Veneto is located in the northeastern part of Italy and it borders Friuli-Venezia Giulia to the east, Emilia Romagna to the south, Lombardia to the west and Trentino-Alto Adige to the north. Its northernmost corner borders Austria.

Veneto is among the wealthiest and most industrial regions in Italy. It has one of Italy's richest historical, cultural, musical, artistic and culinary heritages.

The region is divided into 7 provinces: Venice, Padua, Rovigo, Verona, Vicenza, Treviso and Belluno.

Capital: Venice

Climate

From the cold of the Dolomite mountains to the warmth of the Mediterranean coast, Veneto has a very large range of climate. The climate of Veneto overall is sub-continental, but with the mitigating effect of the sea and the chain of the Alps to protect it from the north winds. The Alpine region has cool summers and cold temperatures in winter with frequent snowfall. Lake Garda is a special case in itself: thanks to its size, the area is relatively mild throughout the year, and has mild winters and very hot summers. In the lower hills and the plains the climate is mild, often with relatively cool wet winters and long hot, sultry summers.

Topography

Veneto has a very diverse landscape, stretching from the Dolomite Mountains in the north to the Adriatic Sea in the east. The northern part of the region is dominated by rugged alpine terrain, with high peaks, deep valleys, and ski resorts, especially around the Belluno province. This area is part of the larger Dolomite range and is known for dramatic mountain scenery and protected natural parks.

Moving south, the land gradually becomes flatter, transitioning into wide plains that form part of the Po Valley, one of Italy's most important agricultural regions. Rivers such as the Brenta, Piave, and Adige flow through the region, shaping fertile farmland and supporting cities and agriculture. In the east, Veneto reaches the Adriatic coast, where you find beaches, lagoons, and the famous Venice Lagoon, a unique landscape of islands, canals, and wetlands.

Origins

Always a rich and prosperous region, after the fall of the Western Empire and the invasion of the Lombards, Veneto was divided for centuries between the continental area, under Lombard rule, and the coastal area, which witnessed the birth of the splendid Republic of Venice from 697.

The feudal system began to break down from the year 1000, and the free communes developed into powerful lordships over the next two centuries. During this period, Ezzelino III da Romano became lord of Verona, Vicenza and Padua. In the following decades, Venetian rule reached the hinterland and many magnificent Palladian villas, designed by architect Palladio for the most important families of the Republic of Venice, were built.

Napoleon and the Austrian rule became a thing of the past after the Third Italian War of Independence in 1866, but during the First World War, Veneto became a battleground once again, with the Piave area remaining a place of resistance for the Italians for months.

Population and Major Cities

Population

As of 2026, the total population is 4.8 million people.

The region covers an area of 18,355 km² and a population density of 264.6/km².

Major Cities (as of 2026)

1. Verona

Verona is best known for its rich Roman and medieval heritage, with one of its most famous landmarks being the well-preserved Verona Arena, an ancient Roman amphitheatre that is still used today for concerts and opera performances. The city is also internationally famous as the setting of Shakespeare’s Romeo and Juliet, attracting visitors to Juliet's Balcony. Beyond its romantic reputation, Verona is recognized for its historic city centre, beautiful piazzas, and important role as a cultural and commercial hub in northern Italy.

Population: 255,139 people

2. Venezia (Venice)

Venice is best known for its unique canal-based structure, where streets are replaced by waterways and transportation is mainly done by boats and gondolas. Built on more than 100 small islands in a lagoon along the Adriatic Sea, the city is famous for landmarks such as St. Mark’s Basilica, the Grand Canal, and its historic bridges. Venice is also world-renowned for its rich artistic heritage, Renaissance architecture, and cultural events like the Venice Carnival and the Venice Film Festival.

Population: 249,385 people

3. Padova (Padua)

Padua is known for its prestigious university, the University of Padua, one of the oldest in the world, where famous figures like Galileo Galilei once taught. The city is also celebrated for its rich artistic and religious heritage, especially the Scrovegni Chapel, which contains breathtaking frescoes by Giotto that are considered masterpieces of Western art. In addition, Padua has a lively historic centre with arcaded streets, piazzas, and one of the largest urban squares in Europe, the Prato della Valle.

Population: 208,202 people

4. Vincenza

Vincenza is best known for its exceptional Renaissance architecture, largely shaped by the famous architect Andrea Palladio, whose designs influenced buildings across Europe and the world. The city is home to several of his masterpieces, including the Basilica Palladiana and the Teatro Olimpico, one of the oldest surviving indoor theatres in existence. Because of this architectural heritage, Vicenza is recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is often called a “city of Palladian architecture.”

Population: 110,741 people

Landmarks

Basilica di San Marco (St Mark's Basilica)

St Mark's Basilica is one of the most iconic landmarks in Venice and a masterpiece of Byzantine architecture. Located in St Mark's Square, it is famous for its golden mosaics, detailed decorations, and impressive domes that reflect the city's historic connection to the Eastern Mediterranean. The basilica was once the private chapel of the Doge and remains a symbol of Venice’s power and artistic heritage.

Grand Canal of Venice

The Grand Canal is the main waterway of Venice and one of the most famous canals in the world. It winds through the city in a large S-shape, lined with historic palaces, churches, and colourful buildings that reflect centuries of Venetian wealth and trade. Acting as the city’s “main street,” it is used by boats, vaporettos, and gondolas, making it essential to everyday life in Venice.

Flag

The flag is comprised of the arms of Regione del Veneto on a Pompeian red background; on the fly edge, seven tails bearing the coat of arms of the seven province capitals of Veneto.

Adoption

The flag was adopted on May 20th, 1975. However, on January 22nd, 1999, the flag was modified.

Symbolism

The flag of Veneto features the Lion of Saint Mark, the historic symbol of Venice and its patron saint, Saint Mark the Evangelist. The lion holds an open gospel displaying the Latin phrase Pax tibi Marce evangelista meus (“Peace to you, Mark, my evangelist”), symbolizing peace, faith, and the region's cultural heritage. Beneath the lion are representations of the Adriatic Sea, the Venetian Plain, and the Alps, reflecting Veneto's diverse geography and the connection between its coast, countryside, and mountains.

Did you know?

Veneto's flag is directly inspired by the banner of the Republic of Venice, a powerful maritime state that lasted for over 1,000 years. Its famous Lion of Saint Mark has been a symbol of Venice since the Middle Ages and remains one of the most recognizable emblems in Italy today. The Republic of Venice controlled important trade routes across the Mediterranean and became one of the wealthiest and most influential states in Europe, helping to spread Venetian culture, art, and architecture throughout the region.

Languages

Veneto's primary language is Italian, which is spoken throughout the region and used in government, education, and daily life. However, many residents also speak Venetian (Veneto), a regional Romance language with its own vocabulary, pronunciation, and literary traditions that developed over centuries.

Although Italian is the official language, Venetian remains an important part of the region's cultural identity and is commonly heard in conversations, local media, and traditional events. The language reflects Veneto's long history, particularly the influence of the former Republic of Venice.

Food

Veneto is known for a diverse and refined cuisine that combines influences from the sea, plains, and mountains. Because of its geography, the region offers everything from seafood dishes in Venice to rice-based and meat dishes in the inland areas, as well as hearty mountain recipes in the northern Dolomites.

It is especially famous for dishes like risotto (especially seafood risottos), polenta, and traditional Venetian specialties such as fegato alla veneziana. Veneto is also widely recognized as the birthplace of Tiramisù, one of Italy's most famous desserts. Overall, its food culture reflects a balance of rich history, regional ingredients, and both land- and sea-based traditions.

Primo (First Course)

Risotto al Nero di Seppia (Squid Ink Risotto)

Risotto al Nero di Seppia is a traditional Venetian dish made with rice cooked in squid ink, giving it a rich seafood flavour and a deep black colour. It is especially popular in coastal areas like Venice, where seafood plays a major role in local cuisine. The dish reflects Veneto's strong connection to the Adriatic Sea and its maritime heritage.

Secondo (Second Course)

Fegato alla Veneziana (Venetian-style Liver)

Fegato alla Veneziana is a classic dish from Venice made with thinly sliced calf's liver cooked with caramelized onions. The sweetness of the onions balances the strong flavour of the liver, creating a simple but traditional recipe. It has been part of Venetian cuisine for centuries and remains a staple in local restaurants.

Dolce (Dessert)

Tiramisù

Tiramisù is one of Italy's most famous desserts and is strongly associated with Veneto, especially the city of Treviso. It is made with layers of coffee-soaked ladyfingers, mascarpone cream, and cocoa powder, creating a rich and creamy texture. The dessert's name means “pick me up,” referring to its energizing mix of coffee and sugar.

Regional Spotlight

Cicchetti

Cicchetti are small bite-sized dishes served in traditional Venetian bars called bacari. They are similar to tapas and can include things like seafood, polenta, cured meats, or small sandwiches. People usually eat them while standing at the bar, often paired with a glass of local wine or an aperitivo. This tradition is a big part of everyday social life in Venice and reflects the region's relaxed, communal food culture.

Art and Music

Did you know?

Veneto is home to the world’s oldest operating café, Caffè Florian in Venice, which opened in 1720. It has welcomed famous visitors such as Casanova, Goethe, and Charles Dickens, making it not only a historic coffeehouse but also a cultural landmark of European literature and art.

Art

Veneto is known for its deep connection to Renaissance art and Venetian painting, especially the works produced during the time of the Republic of Venice. Artists like Titian, Tintoretto, and Veronese helped develop a distinct Venetian style that emphasized rich color, light, and dramatic compositions, influencing European art for centuries. Many of these masterpieces can still be seen in Venice's churches, palaces, and museums today.

Antonio Vivaldi

Antonio Vivaldi was one of the most important composers of the Baroque period and was born in Venice, Veneto. He is best known for his energetic and expressive instrumental music, especially The Four Seasons, a set of violin concertos that vividly portray different times of the year. Vivaldi worked closely with orphaned girls at the Ospedale della Pietà in Venice, where he composed and taught music, helping to shape the city’s strong musical tradition. His work had a major influence on classical music across Europe and remains widely performed today.
