Geography

Abruzzo is a region in Southern Italy located between the Apennine Mountains and the Adriatic Sea. It is known for its diverse landscape, which includes rugged mountains, national parks, rolling hills, and coastal areas. Much of the region is protected as natural parks, making it one of the greenest areas in Europe. Abruzzo is also known for its historic hill towns, traditional cuisine, and strong cultural identity that blends mountain and seaside influences. The region is bordered by Marche to the north, Lazio to the west, Molise to the south, and The Adratic Sea to the east.

Culturally, Abruzzo is considered a region of Southern Italy; however, geographically it is often considered part of Central Italy. Because of its historic association with the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies, the Italian statistical authority ISTAT deems it to be part of Southern Italy.

The region is divided into 4 regions: L'Aquila, Teramo, Pescara, and Chieti.

Capital: L'Aquila (second-largest city of Abruzzo)

Climate

Abruzzo features two distinct, sharply contrasting climates: a Mediterranean maritime climate along the Adriatic coast and a cold continental climate in the inland mountainous interior. While coastal towns enjoy hot, dry summers and mild winters, the Apennine peaks experience heavy winter snowfall and cooler summers.

Summer (June – September): The most popular time to visit. Coastal temperatures hover around 25°C to 29°C (77 to 84°C) but feel highly comfortable due to dry air and a constant sea breeze. Sea temperatures peak in August, making it perfect for swimming

Winter (December – February): Winters are mild and typically range between 7°C and 13°C (45 to 55°F). While it is the rainiest season, prolonged freezing or snowy weather on the coast is rare.

Spring & Autumn: Transition months offer highly pleasant, mild weather ideal for exploring coastal towns and hiking nearby trails.

Topography

Abruzzo is in the center of Italy. It is a land of stunning natural beauty. In 1922–1923, one of the nation's first and most important national parks, the Abruzzo, Lazio and Molise National Park, was created in the region's southwest corner, along its borders with Lazio and Molise (the greater part of the park is in Abruzzo). Since then Abruzzo has been transformed from a poor and remote agricultural zone to a tourist mecca for snow skiers, aquasport enthusiasts, and trekkers who want to challenge its rugged terrain.

Mountains comprise most of the region, and the rest is hilly terrain. The highest spots in the entire Apennine chain are found in Abruzzo. The Corno Grande of the Gran Sasso d'Italia (2,912 m / 9,554 feet) is the tallest mountain between the Alps and Sicily. Other mountains and hills—such as the massive Maiella to the southeast of the Gran Sasso—are separated by plateaus and broad limestone valleys.

Origins

Early Civilisationn

People have been living in Abruzzo since the Neolithic era, with the earliest artefacts found dating back to beyond 6,500 BC. The name Abruzzo, however, is believed to originate from 'Aprutium, coined in the Middle Ages by the Praetutii, an Italic tribe that lived across Central Italy. Not much is known about this group, but their impact can be seen across the region and particularly in Abruzzo where they were primarily based. The word Aprutium is believed to have originated as a combination of the tribe’s name and the title of their chief city, Interamnia, now modern-day Teramo.

Teramo is one of many cities in Abruzzo whose origins date back to ancient civilisation. The small commune of Corfino was the principal city of the Paeligni tribe who lived in the Valle Peligna in Central Abruzzo. Meanwhile, Chieti is believed to be one of the oldest cities in the whole of Italy. Founded by the warring Marrucini tribe, mythology suggests the mythical Greek hero Achilles founded the city and named it Theate, after his mother.

Roman Era

As with surrounding regions, much of Abruzzo’s recognisable history is from the Roman civilisations that ruled the area for much of late-BC and early-AD. Roman generals conquered Teramo between 295 and 290BC, renaming the city Interamnia Praetuttorium, or “City of the Praetutii between two rivers”. Throughout the Roman Era, the community would drop in and out of being a municipality and was hugely successful thanks to its proximity to the empire’s capital, Rome. Thanks to this, the city received many luxuries such as amphitheatres, baths and temples, the remains of which can still be seen today across Teramo.

Historical Influences

In keeping with other regions of Italy, Abruzzo was ruled by many different civilisations following the fall of the Roman Empire, including Lombards, Byzantines and Muslim cultures. The most significant impact in this region of Italy, however, was made by the Normans, particularly their inclusion of the area in the Kingdom of Sicily, the first step to Italian unification in 1860.

Initially, the region was known as Abruzzi, a result of its administration during the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies, when Abruzzo was divided into three parts (known collectively as the Abruzzi). Abruzzi included the modern regions of Abruzzo and Molise to the south, and the two were not split until 1960, which makes Molise the youngest region in Italy. The administrative areas of Abruzzi went on to influence the modern regions of Abruzzo we see today: Abruzzo Citeriore is now Chieti Province in the south; Abruzzo Ulteriore I encompassed modern Teramo and Pescara provinces, and Ulteriore II is now the province of L’Aquila.

World Wars

Abruzzo is also a region with a deep connection to the World Wars that can still be felt today. Abruzzo was part of the Gustav Line, designed to stop Allied forces reaching the stronghold of Rome. The Battle of Ortona took place in December 1943 and is regarded as one of the fiercest campaigns of the war. The coastal town hosts a museum dedicated to the conflict, while the city of Sulmona in Southern Abruzzo is also known for its war memorials.

Population and Major Cities

Population

As of 2026, the population is 1.2 million people.

In addition, the region covers an area of 10,832 km² and has a population density of 117/km².

Major Cities (as of 2026)

1. Pescara

Pescara is the largest city in the Abruzzo region and is located on the Adriatic coast. It is known for its long sandy beaches, modern urban layout, and lively seaside atmosphere. The city serves as an important commercial and transportation hub for the region, with a busy port, airport, and railway connections. Pescara is also famous for its cultural scene and is the birthplace of the Italian poet Gabriele D’Annunzio, which adds historical and literary significance to the city.

Population: 118,487 people

2. L'Aquila

L’Aquila is the capital city of the Abruzzo region, located in the Apennine Mountains. It is known for its medieval history, religious architecture, and mountainous surroundings. The city features important landmarks such as the Basilica of Santa Maria di Collemaggio and the Fountain of the 99 Spouts, reflecting its rich cultural heritage. L’Aquila was heavily damaged by an earthquake in 2009, and since then it has been undergoing long-term restoration and rebuilding efforts. Today, it remains an important cultural and administrative center in the region.

Population: 70,803 people

3. Montesilvano

Montesilvano is a coastal city in the Abruzzo region located just north of Pescara along the Adriatic Sea. It is known for its long beaches, seaside resorts, and tourism-focused economy, especially during the summer months. The city has grown rapidly in recent decades and features a modern urban area near the coast, while the inland part retains more traditional residential neighbourhoods. Montesilvano is a popular destination for visitors looking for a quieter alternative to nearby Pescara.

Population: 53,557 people

4. Teramo

Teramo is a city in the Abruzzo region located between the Gran Sasso mountains and the Adriatic Sea. It is known for its rich history dating back to Roman times, as well as its well-preserved historic center featuring landmarks such as the Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta and Roman ruins. The city has a strong cultural and academic presence, partly due to the University of Teramo. Surrounded by both mountains and countryside, Teramo combines historical heritage with a peaceful, scenic setting.

Population: 51,400 people

Landmarks

Gran Sasso D'Italia

Gran Sasso d'Italia is the highest mountain in the Apennines and one of the most iconic natural landmarks in Italy. Located within the Gran Sasso e Monti della Laga National Park, it is known for its dramatic peaks, hiking trails, and alpine scenery. The area is also important for scientific research, as it hosts one of the largest underground physics laboratories in the world.

Forte Spagnolo

Forte Spagnolo is a large 16th-century fortress located in the city of L’Aquila. Built during Spanish rule, it was designed as a defensive structure to protect the city and assert military control over the region. The fortress is known for its massive stone walls, geometric design, and central courtyard. Today, it also houses the National Museum of Abruzzo, making it both a historic monument and an important cultural site.

Flag

A burgundy field with the coat of arms of Abruzzo in the center.

Adoption

The current flag was adopted on May 21, 1999 (but it was modified on December 27th, 2023).

Symbolism

The flag's background is a bright burgundy colour with gold eyelets in the upper part and gold border around the sides. The tricolour blue, white and green regional coat of arms of Abruzzo is located in the centre. Each colour of the emblem represents different well-known geographical features of the area. White, in the top left of the coat of arms represents snowy mountains. The region is home to the highest peaks of the Apennies mountain range. The green in the centre of the crest represents Abruzzo's hilly area including its three national parks. In the bottom left corner of the emblem, the blue represents the Adriatic sea, which is the sea that borders the left of the region.

These three colours are arranged in the geographical proximity to the coastline. The furthest away are the mountains followed by the hills then finally the sea itself.

Did you know?

The flag of Abruzzo is part of a strict display protocol and is always flown alongside the flags of Italy and the European Union outside official regional buildings. It must be kept in good condition, properly illuminated at night, and lowered to half-mast during moments of mourning, showing the importance of respect and symbolism in regional identity.

Languages

Standard Italian is the official language spoken in Abruzzo, but the region has a deep-rooted linguistic heritage. Alongside Italian, most locals speak Abruzzese, which linguists classify as an Upper Southern Romance language (part of the Neapolitan language family) rather than just an Italian dialect.

Abruzzese: A sister language to Italian, with unique grammar (like using auxiliary verbs based on the person speaking) and vocabulary. It varies by province and town, and it is estimated to have about 1.3 million speakers.

Sabine/Central Dialects: Spoken mainly in the province of L'Aquila, these are closely related to central Italian dialects rather than the southern variants.

Arbëreshë: An Albanian language variety spoken in a few specific ethnic enclaves (such as the town of Villa Badessa) by descendants of historical Albanian immigrants.

Food

Abruzzese cuisine is known for its hearty, traditional dishes that reflect the region’s mountains, countryside, and coastline. Local cooking makes extensive use of lamb, pork, fresh pasta, olive oil, and seasonal vegetables, while seafood is popular in coastal areas along the Adriatic Sea. Many recipes have been passed down through generations and emphasize simple ingredients prepared with care. The region is particularly famous for specialties such as arrosticini, maccheroni alla chitarra, and traditional desserts like parrozzo, making food an important part of Abruzzo’s cultural identity.

Primo (First Course)

Maccheroni alla Chitarra

Maccheroni alla Chitarra is one of Abruzzo’s most famous pasta dishes. Despite its name, it is quite different from the macaroni commonly found in North America, which some people may mistakenly associate it with. The pasta is made using a special tool called a chitarra ("guitar"), whose strings cut the dough into long strands with a distinctive square shape. It is often served with a rich tomato or meat sauce and is considered a symbol of Abruzzo’s culinary tradition.

Secondo (Second Course)

Arrosticini

Arrosticini are skewers of lamb or mutton grilled over an open flame. They are considered one of the signature foods of Abruzzo and are commonly enjoyed at festivals, restaurants, and family gatherings. Their simple preparation highlights the region’s strong pastoral traditions.

Dolce (Dessert)

Parrozzo

Parrozzo is a traditional cake from Pescara made with semolina flour, almonds, and eggs, then coated in chocolate. Created in the early 20th century, it became famous throughout Italy and remains one of Abruzzo’s most iconic desserts.

Regional Spotlight

Seafood

Abruzzo is also known for its seafood cuisine along the Adriatic coast. Fresh fish and shellfish are commonly used in local dishes, especially in coastal towns such as Pescara and Vasto. Traditional recipes often include brodetto, a rich fish stew made with a variety of seafood cooked in tomato and herbs. Seafood plays an important role in balancing the region’s cuisine, which also includes hearty mountain and meat-based dishes from the inland areas.

Art and Music

Art

Art in Abruzzo is mainly reflected through its historic architecture, religious buildings, and traditional craftsmanship. The region is home to many medieval hill towns and churches that feature frescoes and sacred artworks from the Middle Ages and Renaissance periods. Cities such as L’Aquila and Sulmona preserve important cultural landmarks that highlight Abruzzo’s artistic heritage. Traditional crafts, including ceramics and textile work, also play an important role in local culture, showing the region’s connection to rural life and long-standing artistic traditions.

Piero Mazzocchetti

Piero Mazzocchetti is a singer and tenor from Pescara in the Abruzzo region of Italy. He is known for his powerful voice and his style that blends classical opera techniques with modern pop music, often described as popera. Mazzocchetti gained national recognition after performing at the Sanremo Music Festival, where his vocal performance received wide attention. His music often features emotional and melodic compositions, and he has represented the connection between traditional Italian vocal training and contemporary popular music.
